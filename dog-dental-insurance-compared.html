Dog Dental Insurance Compared — Is It Worth It?

📅 June 2025 ⏱️ 8 min read

A routine professional dental cleaning under anaesthesia costs $300–800. A cleaning that uncovers teeth needing extraction can easily reach $1,500–3,000. Yet dental coverage is one of the most misunderstood areas of pet insurance — with exclusions buried in the small print that catch owners off guard at exactly the wrong moment. Here's what you actually need to know before you buy.

Why dental coverage is complicated

Most pet insurance policies treat dental care very differently from other veterinary care. The key distinction almost every policy makes is between dental illness (disease, infection, periodontal disease) and dental accidents (fractured tooth from trauma). Basic policies typically cover only dental accidents. Comprehensive policies may cover dental illness — but almost always with conditions attached, such as requiring a dental exam within a set period of taking out the policy, or excluding pre-existing dental conditions. Periodic professional cleanings (preventive care) are excluded from the vast majority of insurance policies. Some providers offer wellness add-ons that reimburse a portion of preventive dental costs — these are worth examining separately.

The pre-existing condition problem

This is where most owners get caught out. If your dog has any evidence of dental disease at the time you take out a policy — even just minor tartar noted on an exam — that condition will typically be classified as pre-existing and excluded from coverage. For small breeds, which often have dental disease developing by age 3, this means many dogs are already partially or fully excluded by the time their owners think to insure them. The practical implication: If you have a puppy or young dog, especially a high-risk small breed, insure them before their first professional dental exam if possible. Once disease is documented in their records, your coverage options narrow significantly.

What a real claim looks like

To make this concrete: Puffy's second dental surgery — four extractions, full anaesthesia, X-rays, post-operative care — totalled approximately $2,200. Under a typical comprehensive policy (80% reimbursement, $250 annual deductible):

  • Total bill: $2,200
  • Minus deductible: $1,950 claimable
  • 80% reimbursement: $1,560 paid by insurance
  • Owner pays: $640 out of pocket
Without insurance: $2,200 out of pocket. For a Toy Poodle with two major dental surgeries over a lifetime, comprehensive dental insurance typically pays for itself many times over — provided the policy was taken out before disease was documented.

The wellness add-on question

Wellness add-ons that partially reimburse preventive dental cleaning are worth calculating carefully. A typical wellness add-on costs $15–30/month (~$180–360/year) and reimburses $75–200 toward dental cleaning. If your dog needs annual professional cleaning at $400–600, the math can work in your favour — particularly for high-risk small breeds that genuinely need yearly cleanings. However: wellness add-ons rarely cover the full cost of cleaning, and they won't help with the expensive extractions and disease treatment that represent the real financial risk. Don't choose a policy primarily for its wellness dental benefit at the expense of comprehensive illness coverage.

💡 Key Takeaway

Insure early — before dental disease is documented. Look for policies that cover dental illness, not just accidents. For high-risk small breeds, comprehensive coverage with dental illness included is worth the higher premium. Calculate the wellness add-on against your actual preventive cleaning costs.
