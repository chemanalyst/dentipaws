Hi, I'm Oliver

A 9-year-old Toy Poodle with 7 fewer teeth than I should have — and a mission to make sure your dog keeps theirs.

I'm not going to pretend I understood what was happening when I woke up from my first surgery, groggy and sore, with a mouth full of stitches where three of my teeth used to be. I just knew something felt very wrong.

What I know now — what my humans learned the hard way — is that those three teeth didn't have to go. Neither did the four that followed in my second surgery two years later. Seven teeth. Gone. All from a disease that research says is almost entirely preventable.

I'm a Toy Poodle. If you've looked at DentiPaws, you know that puts me in the highest-risk category for dental disease — 26% of Toy Poodles are affected. That's one in four. My humans didn't know that when I was a puppy. They loved me. They fed me quality food, took me to annual checkups, and brushed my teeth when they remembered. Maybe once a week. They thought that was enough.

It wasn't.

How it happened

Years 1 – 4  ·  The quiet beginning

Everything looked fine. It wasn't.

Weekly brushing. Annual vet checkups. A dental chew here and there. My teeth looked okay to the naked eye. What nobody knew was that periodontal disease was already developing below my gumline — in the 60% of my mouth that no one could see without X-rays.

Year 5  ·  First signs

The breath that started the worry.

My humans noticed my breath had changed. Not just normal dog breath — something they described as "fishy" and "off." I started dropping food sometimes when I ate. They finally scheduled a proper dental exam under anesthesia, with X-rays.

Year 6  ·  First surgery

Three teeth extracted. A very hard day.

Severe periodontal disease. Three teeth so damaged they had to come out. My humans cried in the car. I didn't fully understand why, but I remember the recovery clearly — soft food only, pain medication, and them gently stopping me from pawing at my sore mouth.

Years 6 – 8  ·  Too late to fully fix

Everything changed. But the damage was done.

After that surgery, my humans brushed my teeth every single day. VOHC-approved chews. Water additive. Professional cleanings every six months. They did everything right. But the disease that had been silently advancing for four years had already done damage that consistent care couldn't fully reverse.

Year 8  ·  Second surgery

Four more extractions.

The bone loss from those early years had progressed too far. Four more teeth had to go. This time there were no surprises — we knew what to expect — but somehow that made it harder. Another recovery. More stitches. Seven teeth total, gone before my ninth birthday.

Year 9  ·  Today

Living well — but differently.

I manage. Soft food is easier now. Some toys are frustrating. But I'm happy, cared for, and determined that my story means something. My humans don't carry guilt anymore — I've made my peace with that. They didn't know what they didn't know. Now they do. Now you can too.

What I wish my humans had known from day one: As a Toy Poodle, I was at 5 times higher risk than a large breed dog. My tiny jaw crammed a full set of adult teeth into a space too small for them — creating constant plaque traps that I couldn't clean naturally. Daily brushing from puppyhood, combined with 6-monthly professional exams with X-rays, could have caught everything early. When disease is caught early, it's reversible. Once it reaches bone, it isn't.

Those first five years of weekly-when-we-remember brushing set the stage for everything that followed. Four years of under-care became seven extractions.

Why DentiPaws exists

After my second surgery, my human spent weeks reading every piece of veterinary research she could find. She learned that 80% of dogs over 3 have periodontal disease. That only 4% of owners brush daily. That bad breath isn't just "dog breath" — it's bacterial infection. That the disease I had is directly linked to kidney failure and heart valve infections in dogs.

She also found that the information existed — scattered across academic journals and veterinary publications — but no one had made it easy for regular dog owners to find and act on. There was no simple way to say: "Your breed is at high risk. Here's exactly what to do. Here's what to buy."

So she built it. For me. And for every other dog who deserves to keep their teeth.

DentiPaws was built to:

  • Give every breed their own guide — because a Greyhound's risks are not a Lab's risks
  • Give owners immediate, actionable steps — not overwhelming information
  • Flag urgency honestly — some situations need a vet this week, not next year
  • Make daily dental care feel achievable — because 2 minutes a day is genuinely enough
  • Prevent other dogs from losing teeth they didn't have to lose

A letter to your dog's owner

If you're reading this because you Googled your dog's bad breath, or noticed some yellowing, or your vet mentioned "dental disease" at the last visit — you're in the right place, and you're not too late.

The research behind this website studied millions of dogs. The patterns are clear. The preventive measures are proven. Daily brushing works. Early professional exams catch disease when it's still reversible. VOHC-approved products are genuinely effective.

The only thing standing between your dog and what happened to me is starting now. Not when the bad breath gets worse. Not at the next annual vet visit. Now.

I can't get my seven teeth back. But your dog can keep theirs.

Oliver 🐩 Toy Poodle · Age 9 · Seven teeth lighter · Still very much in charge
