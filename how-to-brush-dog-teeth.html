How to Actually Get Your Dog to Accept Tooth Brushing

📅 May 2025 ⏱️ 5 min read

Only 4% of dog owners brush their dog's teeth daily. The most common reason given isn't lack of time or knowledge — it's that the dog won't cooperate. The good news: resistance to brushing is almost always a training problem, not a personality one. With the right approach, the vast majority of dogs can learn to accept — and some to enjoy — daily tooth brushing. Here's the method that works.

Why dogs resist brushing — and why it's fixable

Dogs don't instinctively understand that something going in their mouth repeatedly is beneficial. What they experience, especially the first time, is a strange object, an unfamiliar sensation, and someone restraining their head. Of course they resist. The solution is not to push through the resistance — that creates negative associations that make future brushing harder. The solution is a systematic desensitisation process that builds positive associations at each stage before moving to the next. This process takes 2–4 weeks when done consistently. It requires patience in the early stages, but it creates a dog that genuinely cooperates — making the daily 2-minute brushing routine sustainable for the years and years it needs to happen.

What you'll need

  • Enzymatic dog toothpaste — never human toothpaste (xylitol is toxic to dogs, and fluoride causes stomach issues). Choose a flavour your dog will like — poultry and beef flavours are typically easiest to introduce.
  • A finger brush for the early stages. These slip over your fingertip and are far less threatening than a full toothbrush. Switch to a small angled toothbrush once your dog is comfortable.
  • High-value treats for rewards throughout the process.

The 4-stage desensitisation process

Stage 1 — Touching the muzzle (Days 1–3)
Touch your dog's muzzle gently with your hand for a few seconds. Reward immediately. Repeat several times a session, two sessions a day. The goal is: touch = treat. Nothing else yet. Stage 2 — Lips and gums with your finger (Days 4–7)
Gently lift the lips and touch the outer gum surface with a bare finger. Reward immediately. Gradually increase the duration. Once comfortable, dip your finger in toothpaste and repeat — let them lick it, then touch the gums. Still no brush. Stage 3 — Finger brush introduction (Days 8–14)
Put the finger brush on, apply toothpaste, and let your dog sniff and lick it. Then gently move it along a few teeth on the outer surface — just a few seconds. Reward. Gradually extend the time and coverage over several days. Focus on the outer surfaces only — that's where 90% of the cleaning benefit happens. Stage 4 — Full brushing routine (Days 15+)
Work up to covering all outer tooth surfaces in a gentle circular motion. Pay particular attention to the back upper premolars and molars — these are the teeth most prone to tartar accumulation in most breeds. The full routine should take 2 minutes. Reward consistently, every session.

Tips for persistent resistance

Go slower, not harder. If your dog is resisting at any stage, you've moved too fast. Go back one stage and spend more time there. Always end on a positive. If a session is going badly, stop and do something easy that earns a treat — don't end on resistance. Try different times of day. Many dogs accept brushing better after exercise when they're calmer. Others prefer it as part of the morning routine. Two-person technique for very resistant dogs. One person holds and reassures while the other brushes. This works well for small breeds who wriggle. For truly resistant dogs: Some dogs need a slower process or professional guidance. Ask your vet about referral to a veterinary behaviourist if resistance is severe. In the meantime, dental gels, water additives, and VOHC-approved chews provide partial benefit while you continue working on brushing acceptance.

Maintaining the habit

The biggest enemy of daily brushing is not resistance — it's forgetting. Once you've built the routine, protecting it matters. Set a phone alarm. Attach it to another daily habit — after dinner is a popular and effective pairing since food is already on the dog's mind. Put the toothbrush somewhere visible in your routine path. Research shows that owners who brush at the same time every day are significantly more likely to maintain the habit long term. Consistency matters more than perfection — a brushing that covers 70% of the teeth every day is far more valuable than a perfect brushing three times a week.

💡 Key Takeaway

Resistance to brushing is a training issue, not a character flaw. The desensitisation process takes 2–4 weeks and creates lasting cooperation. Go slowly, reward consistently, and never end on a negative. Same time every day protects the habit.
